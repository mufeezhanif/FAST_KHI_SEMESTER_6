Genre of Writing

Four Language Skills & Writing

Receptive Skills

Listening and Reading — the person receives information from an external source.

Productive Skills

Speaking and Writing — the person produces the message themselves.

💡

Writing as a Productive Skill: A writer is the originator of the message. Unlike copying or transcribing (non-productive), creative and technical writing requires the writer to generate, structure, and communicate their own ideas.

Why Writing Matters

  • Records history and preserves culture, religion, and ideology.
  • Guides and instructs others effectively.
  • Codifies language, making it permanent.
  • Carries immense power — written word is far more reliable than oral communication.
  • Is the most appropriate medium in most professional situations.

Main Genres of Writing

Fiction

  • Poetry
  • Novel / Novelette / Novella
  • Short Story
Non-Fiction
  • Essays
  • Reports

  • Memos, Articles, Editorials
  • Reviews, Letters

Five Types of Writing

Type Examples Key Trait
Creative Writing Poems, plays, stories Connotative/expressive language, fictional characters, imagery, plots
Expressive Writing Narratives, descriptions Subjective, personal experience-based, connotative words
Expository Writing Comparison, analysis, cause/effect, argument Objective; uses both connotative and denotative words
Journalism News stories, features, editorials Objective, factual, short sentences, mostly denotative

📌

Exam Tip: Know the shoe example! A technical spec describes the D40 Slammer with exact measurements. A poem about the same shoe uses imagery and emotion. Both describe a shoe — but the genre completely changes the language and purpose.

What is Technical Writing?

📖

Definition: A specialized field of communication whose purpose is to convey technical and scientific information and ideas accurately and efficiently. It deals with subjects in engineering, business, trade, government, technology, and science.

The goal of technical writing is to enable readers to use a technology or understand a process or concept. The writer's voice is secondary — the subject matter is primary. Style is direct and utilitarian, emphasizing exactness and clarity over elegance.

Four Major Categories of Technical Documents

📊 Day-to-Day Business Reports

Progress reports, recommendation reports, memos, inquiry letters, emails within and outside the organization.

📚 Education & Knowledge Sharing

Technical papers, magazine articles, books, theses — for teaching and sharing knowledge.

📜 Patents

Highly specific documents that describe the purpose, specs, dimensions, parts, and power source of inventions.

📋 Operational Manuals

Instructions and procedures for using, assembling, repairing, or maintaining products and systems.

History of Technical Writing

Prehistoric Era — Cave paintings in France and Spain illustrated hunting techniques, representing the earliest form of instructional/technical communication.

Babylonians — Clay tablets with technical information on astronomy, mathematics, agriculture, and instruction manuals for making beer.

Ancient Egypt — Papyrus writings in medicine and mathematics.

Ancient Greeks — Most prolific early technical writers; writings on mathematics, biology, psychology, and physical sciences laid foundations for Western civilization.

16th Century — First manual on military weapons marked the start of modern instructional writing.

World War II — Tremendous acceleration in technical writing; needed to explain new devices and weapons quickly to non-scientists and soldiers.

Today — Technical writers explain systems, consumer products, and services generated by advances in computer science, engineering, agriculture, biology, chemistry, and physics.

Importance of Technical Writing

Technical writing is not just a skill — it is a professional necessity. Here's why:

Constitutes significant workload — Engineers, scientists, and developers write progress reports, recommendation reports, memos, inquiry letters daily.

Facilitates communication — With co-workers, clients, and supervisors both inside and outside the organization. Technical skills alone are not enough.

Necessary for career success — Organizations judge engineers partly by how well they write. Poorly written docs reveal inefficiency. Engineers who communicate clearly get promoted.

Enables product use and safety — Without clear specs/directions, products can be dangerous. TW prevents misuse and reduces liability.

Required for patents — A patent must clearly describe the machine's purpose, dimensions, parts, and power source using technical writing.

Reduces costs and errors — Well-written manuals reduce downtime, training costs, and support costs.

Functions of Technical Writing

  • Serve as basis for management decisions
  • Furnish needed information
  • Give instructions
  • Explain techniques
  • Report achievements
  • Analyze problem areas
  • Determine design and system requirements
  • Serve as basis for public relations
  • Provide report to stockholders
  • Develop a product
  • Provide service
  • Record business proposals
  • Procure business through proposals

Technical Writing in Computer Science

CS careers demand well-developed technical writing skills from day one — starting with your CV and cover letter.

📁 Project Proposals

Introduce projects to clients/management. Identify the problem and present the solution. Must convince audience of viability.

📄 POS — Project Overview Spec

First refinement of proposal. Mutual understanding between dev team and client. Includes goals, budget, schedule, constraints.

📌 SPMP — Software Project Management Plan

Describes management process, risks, schedules, budget, tools. Focuses on how the project is managed, not the software itself.

📃 SRS — Software Requirements Specification

Completely defines functionality via use-cases. Includes non-functional requirements (security, performance, reliability). Acts like a contract.

🏗 SDS / SDD — Software Design Spec

Describes system architecture, how modules interact. Uses diagrams. Details design decisions for maintainability.

🧪 STS & STR — System Test Specs & Report

Plans for testing software. Specifies test cases and procedures. Results compiled into a System Test Report evaluating requirements satisfaction.

📙 User Manuals

Step-by-step guides for using software. Easy to follow, targeted to end users.

💬 Code Comments

Allow other developers to understand intended behavior. Critical for modification and maintenance.

📧 Memoranda (Memos)

Fulfill a variety of objectives within organizations. Style depends on purpose. Usually a few pages with clear intent.

02

Style of Technical Writing

📖

Style Defined: "Proper words in the proper places" (Jonathan Swift) / "Dress of thoughts" (Seneca). In TW, it's how a writer arranges words, sentences, and paragraphs to communicate thoughts clearly to a specific audience.

10 Core Attributes of Technical Writing

Pertains to a technical subject

Has a clear purpose

Has a measurable objective

Conveys information, facts, and data

Is impersonal in nature

Is concise — no wasted words

Is directed at a specific audience

Uses a particular style and format

Is archival — kept for record

Cites contributions of others

1. Language

1.1 Formal English

Technical writing uses formal, direct language. Avoid items that undermine professionalism:

❌ Colloquialisms

Local/regional expressions imitating informal speech.
"gonna" → "going to"; "passed on" → "died"

⚠️ Jargon / Slang

Terminology specific to a group. If used, always define it. "run" (computer simulation), "hang on" (wait)

🚫 Clichés

Worn-out expressions that have lost their original meaning. "Bite the bullet," "in the long run," "avoid like the plague"

Cliché Replacement Guide

Cliché Replacement
Bite the bullet Sacrifice
Don't mince words Be precise
Drop in the bucket Tiny
In this day and age Today
Ins and outs Details
No mean feat Difficult
Only time will tell Eventually
Whets the appetite Invites
⚖️

Sexist Language: Avoid gender-specific words for roles that can be either gender. Do not use he/she (disrupts text flow). Instead, use plurals, restructure the sentence, or alternate he and she.

1.2 Simplicity

Use technical words only when necessary. Avoid gobbledygook — unintelligible, pompous, stiff language.

Jargonized / Pompous Simple & Formal
We will use the input of each department to finalize our game plan. We will consider the suggestions of each department to complete our programming.
At this juncture, the aforementioned procedure should be utilized. The plan we discussed should be used now.
We should commence operational capabilities in systematic increments. We should begin the project step by step.

1.3 Objectivity

Technical writing is characterized by objectivity and impersonality. Avoid personal, subjective, emotionally stimulating, and judgmental tone. Objectivity establishes credibility.

❌ Subjective
  • "The results of the tests were incredibly wonderful."
✅ Objective
  • "Ninety percent of the tested samples met the accepted criteria."
📌

To achieve objective, impersonal style: use passive voice and third-person point of view in scientific writing where appropriate. However, don't overdo it — active voice is generally preferred for clarity.

1.4 Direct Language

Every word must have a place and a meaning. Use direct statements and active voice. Avoid past tense except in executive summaries. Use future tense for projections.

1.5 Negatives — Use Positive Form

Prefer positive declarations over negative ones. They are more direct and promote precise language.

Negative Form Positive Form
Do not include Exclude
Do not permit Forbid
Do not allow Disallow

1.6 Simple Terms

Impress with analysis, not vocabulary. Replace complex words with simpler ones that convey the same meaning. "Utilize" → "use"; "altercation" → "dispute".

1.7 Action Verbs

Replace weak verb-preposition combinations with precise action verbs: go with → accompany; find out → discover; start out → begin.

1.8 Concreteness

Be specific, definite, and vivid rather than vague. Use denotative (direct, dictionary-based) rather than connotative words.

Vague → Concrete
"Student GMAT scores are higher."
"In 1996, the GMAT score averaged 600; by 1997 they had risen to 610."
Bland → Vivid
"This is a long letter."
"This letter is three times as long as you said it would be."

2. Conciseness

Definition: Saying what you want to say in the fewest possible words without sacrificing important information. A concise message is complete without being wordy.

Conciseness can be achieved at two levels:

  • Limit paragraph length
  • Limit word and sentence length

Techniques for Conciseness

📌 Replace Abstract Nouns with Verbs
Wordy → Concise
"The acquisition of the property was accomplished through long and hard negotiations."
"The property was acquired through long and hard negotiation."

"Confirmation of the contract occurred yesterday."
"The contract was confirmed yesterday."
📌 Use Short Words (1-2 syllable preference)
Long Word Short Word
cognizant know
endeavor try
domicile home
morbidity death
terminate end
utilize use
📌 Avoid Redundancy
Wordy Concise
We collaborated together on the project. We collaborated on the project.
This is a brand new innovation. This is an innovation.
The other alternative is to eat soup. The alternative is to eat soup.
📌 Avoid Prepositional Phrases
Wordy Concise
I will see you in the near future. I will see you soon.
He drove at a rapid rate. He drove rapidly.
📌 Avoid Passive Voice (where possible)
"The window was broken by the boys." (7 words, weak verb "was")
"The boys broke the window." (5 words, strong verb "broke")
📌 Avoid Shun Words (-tion / -sion endings)
Shun Word Concise Version
Came to the conclusion Concluded / decided
With the exception of Except for
Make revisions Revise
Consider implementation Implement
📌 Avoid Camouflaged Words
Camouflaged Concise
Make an amendment to Amend
Have a meeting Meet
For the purpose of discussing Discuss
Thanking you in advance Thank you
📌 Avoid Expletive Pattern

Avoid starting sentences with There is/are/was/were or It is/was.

"There are three people who will work for Acme."
"Three people will work for Acme."

3. Clarity

The most important criterion for effective technical writing. If a reader responds with "Huh?" — the writer has failed. Lack of clarity can lead to:

😟

BAD — Equipment damaged; reader returns it; costs and bad PR.

🤕

WORSE — Owner is hurt: pain, anxiety, doctor's bills, bad PR.

⚖️

EVEN WORSE — Company is sued; writer loses job.

How to Achieve Clarity

Provide Specific Detail: Use quantified, specific information. Replace vague adjectives (some, recently, good) with exact numbers and dates.

Answer the Reporter's Questions: Who? What? When? Where? Why? How? — Every important technical document should address all six.

Use Familiar Words: Avoid obscure words. Replace accede with "agree," inasmuch as with "because," ascertain with "find out," cognizant with "know."

Clear Pronoun Reference: Ensure every pronoun clearly refers to a specific noun. When in doubt, repeat the noun to avoid ambiguity (especially in safety-critical writing).

Text Flow — Creating Coherent Paragraphs

2.1 Coherent Paragraphs

Each paragraph = one central topic. ~5 sentences. Place key topic at start of sentence, new information at end. Arrange facts from general to specific. Link paragraphs by previewing the next one.

2.2 Transitional Words

Connect ideas within and between paragraphs. Examples: earlier/later (time); next (sequence); in conclusion (end); also/but (compare/contrast); because/therefore (causality); furthermore/similarly/moreover (addition).

2.3 Sentence Variety

Vary length and structure. Avoid very short or excessively long sentences. Use Simple, Compound, Complex, and Compound-Complex sentences for flow. Simplify verb phrases for non-native readers.

2.4 Acronyms and Initialisms

Acronym = pronounced as a word (RAM, scuba). Initialism = pronounced letter by letter (IBM). First appearance: write full name then abbreviation in parentheses. Plural: add a lowercase s. Possessive: add 's.

4. Accuracy

Errors in technical writing make the company and employee look bad. More critically, errors can cause damages, injuries, lawsuits, or serious misunderstandings.

📄 Document Accuracy

Proper coverage of topics in appropriate detail. Requires a clear problem statement and preliminary outline.

✍️ Stylistic Accuracy

Careful use of language to express meaning. Involves paragraph/sentence structure and word choice.

🔬 Technical Accuracy

Grounded in technical mastery of the subject. Represents data with minimum distortion. Depends on conceptual command of vocabulary.

Proofreading Techniques

  • Use spell check — but remember: it won't catch form/from, to/too, except/accept.
  • Let it sit — review after a day or weekend for fresh eyes.
  • Use peer evaluations — others spot errors you miss.
  • Read it aloud — you can often hear errors.
  • Read it backwards — removes context so you catch individual word errors.

5. Numbers — Rules

Spell out numbers 1–10 in isolation: "The discussion lasted ten minutes."

Spell out indefinite numbers expressible in 1–2 words: "Approximately thirty appliances were damaged."

Spell out a number that starts a sentence. If awkward, restructure the sentence.

Use numerals for exact numbers above 10: "The corporate file has been missing for 31 days."

Consistent usage: If several numbers perform similar functions in a sentence, express them all as figures. "21 ranges, 9 refrigerators, 37 washers, 10 dryers."

Spell out million/billion when rounding: "$7 million."

6. That vs. Which

"That" — Restrictive (no comma)

Defines and restricts; the information is critical to the meaning.

"The article that was printed yesterday is inaccurate."
"Which" — Non-Restrictive (with comma)

Adds descriptive, incidental information; preceded by a comma, dash, or parenthesis.

"She wore a baseball cap, which is unexpected for a ballerina."

7. Patterns of Organization

🗺 Spatial

Used for describing physical objects or spaces. Organize left to right, top to bottom, inside to outside, clockwise. Good for architectural or mechanical descriptions.

📅 Chronological

Used to document time or steps in a procedure. Essential for lab reports, procedures, emergency reports, step-by-step instructions.

⭐ By Importance

Most important ideas at the top. Page space is like real estate — bury key data and readers miss it. Used in agendas, recommendations.

⚖️ Comparison / Contrast

Compare similarities, contrast differences. Common in business proposals when evaluating options or competing products.

🔧 Problem / Solution

Every proposal is problem/solution oriented. Identify the problem, then present your solution, benefits, and why it's better than alternatives.

The Technical Writing Process

💡

Key Insight: Technical writing is a recursive process — not linear. Earlier sections may need revision as new sections are completed. Begin writing components as you complete phases of your work project, so parts are ready to revise when you're done with the underlying task.

The Three Main Phases

📋 Planning
  • Analyzing purpose
  • Analyzing audience
  • Determining document type
  • Analyzing writing situation
  • Collecting data
✍️ Drafting
  • Writing first draft
  • Organizing content
  • Creating text flow
  • Adding visuals/structure
🔄 Revising
  • Editing for grammar
  • Editing for mechanics
  • Peer review
  • Final proofreading

Analyzing Purpose

A document has two purposes:

Writer's Purpose
  • Why is the writer writing?
  • What does the writer want the reader to know and do?
Reader's Purpose
  • Why is the reader reading?
  • What does the reader want to know or do?

The 6 Writer Purposes

1. To Instruct Purpose Type

Teach how to perform a task. Cover: purpose, steps, conditions, warnings.
Examples: Training manuals, operator manuals, consumer instructions

2. To Record Purpose Type

Document tests, research results, decisions made, responsibilities assigned, actions and their consequences.
Examples: Minutes, file reports, lab reports

3. To Inform (for Decision Making) Purpose Type

Provide accurate data and thorough analysis to enable the reader to make decisions. Specific facts required.
Examples: Progress reports, feasibility reports, investigative reports

4. To Inform (without Decision Making) Purpose Type

Explain the who, what, where, when, why, and how. Show cause and effect. Relate information to company interest.
Examples: Bulletins, literature reviews, product descriptions

5. To Recommend Purpose Type

Provide reasons, expected benefits, and explain why the recommendation is preferable to alternatives.
Examples: Simple proposals, feasibility studies, recommendation reports

6. To Persuade Purpose Type

Provide sound evidence, counter-arguments, importance of action, consequences of inaction, and benefits.
Examples: Construction bids, grant applications, technical news releases

Analyzing the Writing Situation

No writer works in isolation. Consider the organizational environment, communication atmosphere, government regulations, professional standards, etc. Ask:

  • Is the subject controversial within the organization?
  • What events created the need for this document?
  • What continuing events depend on this document?
  • Given the deadline, what information can realistically be included?
  • What influence will this document have on company operations or goals?
  • Is the subject controlled by government regulations?
  • What external groups are involved, and why?

Analyzing the Audience

🎯

For most technical writers, audience analysis is the most important consideration in planning, writing, and reviewing a document. You "adapt" your writing to meet the needs, interests, and background of your readers.

Categories of Technical Readers

Reader Type Reading Purpose & Needs
Executives Make decisions based on applicability and profitability. Want conclusions and alternatives, not details.
Technologists, Engineers, Scientists Information transfer. Need facts, details, theory, methodology, and conclusions.
Technicians Troubleshoot, modify, upgrade, maintain equipment. Need practical information in easy-to-use format with visuals.
Operators Operate equipment or perform procedures. Need step-by-step commands with visuals.
Non-technical Persons Read for interest and information. Require simple language, glossaries, summaries.

6 Key Questions for Audience Analysis

Q1 — Who are my specific readers?
External
  • Customers, vendors, stockholders
  • Government agencies
  • Industry associations
  • General public
Internal
  • Supervisors (decision makers)
  • Subordinates (lower rank)
  • Peers (equals)
Q2 — Why do they need this document?

What specific information does the reader need? Why do they need it? What material is appropriate and in what sequence?

Q3 — How will they use it?

Technical documents are rarely read beginning to end. Readers access specific sections based on their needs. Design accordingly (headings, index, abstracts).

Q4 — What is their attitude? (Hostile / Friendly / Neutral)

A hostile reader needs persuasion techniques — organize from generally accepted to less accepted data. A reluctant reader needs lists, headings, and emphasis on importance.

Q5 — What is their technical knowledge level?
Level Characteristics
Expert Few definitions needed. Requires detailed technical data, methodology.
Semi-Expert Variable knowledge. Needs more definitions and explanations of general principles.
Non-Expert (Lay) No specialized training. Needs glossaries of terms, checklists, simple graphics, and summaries.

Primary vs. Secondary Readers

Primary Readers
  • The intended audience
  • Will act or decide based on document
  • Can be one or many
Secondary Readers
  • May be affected or influenced
  • Not the original audience
  • May include lawyers, other departments

Strategies for Multiple Primary Readers

  • Use headings that direct different readers to relevant sections.
  • Write a different cover letter emphasizing relevant sections for each reader type.
  • Sometimes write separate reports for different audiences.
04

Synthesis Writing

📖

Definition: A synthesis is a written discussion that incorporates support from several sources of differing views. It identifies relationships between sources through common themes or traits, and creates new knowledge out of existing knowledge.

A synthesis essay combines sources with the writer's own position to form a cohesive, supported argument, with accurate citations. It differs from a simple argumentative paper by focusing more on relationships between sources than on a single point. It is closer to a compare-and-contrast paper in structure.

🚫

What synthesis is NOT: It is not a reflective or narrative paper. First-person writing and subjective opinion are not acceptable.

Types of Synthesis Essays

📊 Explanatory Synthesis

Helps readers understand a complex topic. Present facts, data, and different perspectives — no argument needed. Common in research papers, literature reviews, and scientific articles.

⚔️ Argumentative Synthesis

Supports a controversial position using data from multiple sources. Like an argumentative paper but considers the relationships between references, especially opposing views.

📚 Review Synthesis

Summary of research to discover previous findings on a topic. Instead of proving a point, you examine ideas. Thesis may simply state that the issue needs more research.

Integrating Source Evidence

There are three main ways to integrate evidence from sources. All require citation because you are using another person's words and/or ideas — even if you paraphrase.

💬 Quoting

Using the exact words of the original source in quotation marks. Must cite.

🔄 Paraphrasing

Rewording a specific passage in your own words. Close in length to the original. Must cite.

📋 Summarizing

Condensing a large chunk of text to capture only main ideas. Much shorter than original. Must cite.

When to Use Direct Quotations

  • When the original phrasing/imagery is irreplaceable and no paraphrase could be as effective.
  • When the language itself is the focus of your analysis (e.g., analyzing rhetoric, metaphor, or specific phrasing).
⚠️

Never "quote and run." A quotation does not speak for itself. Always use the I.C.E. method: Introduce → Cite → Explain. Never leave your reader to determine the relevance of a quotation without your analysis.

The "Quotation Sandwich"

INTRODUCE

Set up the quotation: provide context, name the author, explain what's happening

CITE

Include the quotation with proper citation (in-text reference)

EXPLAIN

Analyze, explain, or interpret how the quotation supports your argument

Three Grammatical Integration Methods

Method 1: Seamless Integration

Embed quoted words as an organic part of your own sentence. No signal phrase needed — the quote flows naturally.

Dickens begins his novel with the paradoxical observation that the eighteenth century was both "the best of times" and "the worst of times" [1].
Method 2: Signal Phrase Method

Use a signal phrase (Author + Verb) to introduce the quotation, clearly indicating its source.

Describing the eighteenth century, Charles Dickens observes, "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times" [1].
Method 3: Colon Method

When your introductory words form a complete sentence, use a colon to introduce and set off the quotation (gives it added emphasis).

Dickens defines the eighteenth century as a time of paradox: "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times" [1].

Editing Quotations (When Needed)

[ Square Brackets ]

Use to show changes or additions you've made to a quote to fit your sentence grammatically.

Petroski observed that "[e]ngineers are always striving for success…" [3].
… Ellipses (3 or 4 dots)

3 dots = words removed within a sentence. 4 dots = substantial text removed, including a sentence-ending period.

"Planners expected that when completed the … bridge would set a world record…" [3].

Paraphrase vs. Summary

Paraphrase
  • Focuses on a specific, small section
  • May be close in length to original
  • Rewrites in your own words
  • Demonstrates detailed understanding
Summary
  • Condenses a large chunk of text
  • Much shorter than original
  • Captures only main ideas
  • Useful for giving overview
📌

Important: Both paraphrase and summary require citations. You are still using someone else's intellectual property — words, ideas, language structures, images, statistics, data, or formulas.

Signal Verbs

Avoid overusing vague verbs like "says," "writes," or "discusses." Choose signal verbs that indicate the author's rhetorical purpose — what is the author doing in that passage?

Verbs of Assertion

argues, claims, asserts, insists, contends, maintains, declares

Verbs of Analysis

analyzes, examines, explores, investigates, compares, contrasts, evaluates

Verbs of Description

describes, illustrates, portrays, depicts, characterizes, presents

Verbs of Agreement

agrees, confirms, supports, affirms, concedes, acknowledges

Verbs of Suggestion

suggests, proposes, recommends, urges, advises, cautions, warns

Verbs of Explanation

explains, clarifies, defines, notes, observes, points out, reveals

⚠️ Grammar Rule: "That" After Signal Verbs

Some verbs can be directly followed by "that"; others require an object first.

✅ Verbs that take "that" directly

thinks, believes, expects, decides, knows, understands, says, admits, argues, agrees, claims, explains, suggests, etc.

"She argues that bottled water should be banned."
❌ Verbs that need an object first

tell, describe, convince, persuade, inform, remind, analyze, summarize, support

"The author persuades that…" ✗
"The author persuades readers that…" ✓
🚫

Common Errors to Avoid:
✗ "The author persuades that…"
✗ "The writers convince that…"
✗ "He analyzes that…"
✗ "She informs that…"
✗ "They described that…"
These are grammatically incorrect constructions.

Sample Paraphrase

At the end of its construction, the large cantilever bridge cost $25 million dollars, but the cost in lives far exceeded the prediction of one death per million spent. While the planners hoped the bridge would set a global record, its claim to fame was much more grim [3].

Sample Summary

According to Petroski, a large bridge built in Quebec during the early twentieth century claimed the lives of dozens of workers during its construction. The collapse early in construction represented a pivotal design failure for Canadian engineers that shaped the profession [3].

Writing a Synthesis Essay — Quick Guide

Read all sources carefully. Identify the main argument, evidence, and tone of each source. Note where they agree and disagree.

Develop your thesis. Your central argument must be your own — sources exist to support you, not replace you. Write a clear, debatable thesis statement.

Group sources by theme. Identify relationships between sources (agreement, disagreement, partial overlap). This forms the backbone of your essay structure.

Integrate evidence using I.C.E. Introduce → Cite → Explain. Never quote and run. After every piece of evidence, explain its relevance to your argument.

Cite both direct and indirect citations. Any use of another's words or ideas — whether quoted, paraphrased, or summarized — requires a citation.

Use appropriate signal verbs. Choose verbs that accurately represent the author's rhetorical purpose. Check grammar: does this verb need an object before "that"?

Conclude by synthesizing. Reinforce your argument; do not simply summarize. Show how the sources collectively support your position or reveal a tension that you resolve.
